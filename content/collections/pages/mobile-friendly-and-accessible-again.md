      Your website should work wherever it is viewed.

      For most businesses, the majority of visitors are now on phones. If your site only works properly on a desktop monitor, you are losing attention, trust, and enquiries without realising it.

      Mobile issues are often subtle. Buttons are too small. Text feels cramped. Navigation becomes awkward. Forms are frustrating to complete. Nothing is completely broken, but the experience feels harder than it should.

      That friction adds up.

    title: 'Fixing layouts that break on smaller screens'
    content: |-
      A site that "shrinks" is not the same as a site that is truly mobile-friendly.

      Common mobile problems include:

      - Fixed-width layouts that do not adapt properly
      - Content overlapping or spilling off the screen
      - Navigation menus that are difficult to use
      - Images that push important content too far down
      - Forms that are hard to complete on touch devices    

      Sometimes the fix is adjusting styles and layout rules. Sometimes it means rebuilding sections properly so content flows naturally across screen sizes.

      The goal is consistency. Your website should feel considered on every device, not like an afterthought.

    title: 'Designing for real usage'
    content: |-
      Mobile visitors behave differently.

      They skim more. They scroll more. They are often distracted or in a hurry. That means clarity matters even more.

      Part of making a site mobile-friendly is:

      - Simplifying layouts
      - Prioritising important content
      - Reducing visual clutter
      - Making calls to action obvious and easy to tap    

      It is not about making the mobile version smaller. It is about making it clearer.

    title: 'Accessibility as a practical improvement'
    content: |-
      Accessibility is sometimes treated as a compliance task. In reality, it is about usability.

      Improving accessibility typically includes:

      - Clear heading structure
      - Sufficient colour contrast
      - Meaningful alternative text for images
      - Forms that can be used with a keyboard
      - Logical focus order
      - Descriptive link text    

      These changes help people using assistive technologies, but they also make your site easier for everyone.

      Clearer structure improves readability. Better contrast improves legibility. Thoughtful form design reduces errors.

      Accessibility and usability go hand in hand.

      - Less likelihood of costly fixes later    

      It is far easier to improve structure and usability proactively than to retrofit it under pressure.
